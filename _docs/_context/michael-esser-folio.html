Philosophy
"The best products emerge when you deeply understand both the technology and the humans it serves—then find the leverage points where small changes create outsized impact."

My path to product management wasn't linear. I started in healthcare and community education, learning firsthand how systems—whether for patient intake or training programs—either empower or frustrate the people using them. That foundation in human-centered operations led me to technical support, then engineering, and finally to product ownership. Each step taught me something essential: how to listen to what users actually need, how systems break under real-world pressure, and how to translate between business goals and technical constraints.

I'm obsessed with finding leverage. Whether it's introducing AI-assisted workflows that double engineering velocity or modernizing legacy systems that unlock new capabilities, I look for the interventions that create disproportionate impact. I believe product managers should be technical enough to understand the architecture and empathetic enough to feel the user's frustration. That combination is what lets you make the right tradeoffs—and ship things that actually matter.

How I work
01
Start With the Constraints

Every interesting product problem has a constraint at its core—technical debt, team capacity, user behavior, or market timing. I've learned to identify that constraint first, because solving the wrong problem quickly still wastes everyone's time. The best solutions often come from reframing the constraint itself.

02
Translate, Don't Dictate

Coming from engineering, I know how frustrating it is when product requirements feel disconnected from technical reality. I invest heavily in understanding what's actually hard, then work with teams to find solutions that satisfy both user needs and engineering sanity. The goal is shared understanding, not requirements documents that gather dust.

03
Measure What Moves

I've seen too many dashboards full of metrics nobody acts on. When I led analytics integrations, I focused on the numbers that would actually change decisions. If a metric doesn't have a clear 'if X then Y' attached to it, it's probably not worth tracking—at least not prominently.

04
Multiply Through Tools

The 2–3x velocity improvement I achieved through AI workflows wasn't magic—it came from systematically identifying where developers were spending time on low-leverage work, then finding tools and processes that eliminated that friction. I'm always looking for these multipliers, whether they're AI-powered or just better documentation.
